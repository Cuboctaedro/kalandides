Earlier pieces addressed phenomenology, humanistic geography, and relational approaches to space and power. Here the focus is on the relation between time, space, and embodiment, drawing primarily on phenomenological accounts of temporality associated with Husserl and Heidegger, together with work on presence and the spatial-temporal organisation of experience. These approaches have been important for me because they make it possible to think of human experience as unfolding within configurations of rhythm, duration, movement, and orientation, rather than as a set of isolated mental contents.</p>"},"id":"edf6e13e-e347-4c1f-8e08-78357c4a77a3","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Husserl’s analyses of internal time-consciousness remain significant here. His account of temporality shows that lived experience is never confined to a punctual present. What is given now is always accompanied by retentions of what has just passed and protentions directed toward what is about to come. The present therefore has thickness. It carries traces of prior experience and anticipations of what is imminent. This has implications for how environments are lived. A room is never encountered as a purely immediate setting. It is already saturated with memory, expectation, and affective anticipation. A workplace in which one has repeatedly felt scrutinised is entered with a sedimented temporal orientation; the body already expects interruption or judgement. A home associated with rest is inhabited with a different temporal structure, one that supports a loosening of vigilance and a different distribution of attention.</p>"},"id":"cadc357b-b8c0-47b6-90f6-467777ea558f","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Heidegger’s account of temporality deepens this line of thought by locating human existence within an ongoing structure of thrownness, projection, and situated involvement in the world. Human beings always find themselves already somewhere, within a set of inherited conditions, rhythms, obligations, and possibilities. Time is not simply a sequence of moments through which the subject passes. It is constitutive of how one’s world appears and how possibilities become available or recede. Spatiality and temporality are intertwined within this structure. The places in which a person dwells, works, waits, moves, or returns are inseparable from the temporal horizons through which they interpret their situation. The significance of a town, a home, or a workplace depends partly on how it is located within a life trajectory, whether it is felt as opening a future, prolonging a stagnation, or carrying a past that remains active.</p>"},"id":"6e5e3859-cd4e-4e1b-bdca-263c871bb5b2","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>This understanding of temporality informs my coaching in a direct way. I am interested in how people inhabit time spatially and space temporally. When a coachee describes feeling stuck, hurried, delayed, or out of sync, I ask where those experiences occur and how those places organise their sense of time. Some environments accelerate perception and narrow attention. Others allow for duration, hesitation, or thought. The pace of a workplace, the structure of a commute, the rhythm of a household, the social expectations embedded in a neighbourhood, the temporal habits of a family or community—these all shape how a person experiences what is urgent, what can wait, what feels already closed, and what remains open.</p>"},"id":"7b481b47-fbda-4937-9c95-d90bd8b04ed6","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>For this reason, coaching cannot treat time as an abstract resource to be managed independently of setting and embodiment. People do not simply “have” more or less time. They inhabit temporal orders that are materially and spatially organised. A person living in a crowded home may have very little access to uninterrupted time because privacy is scarce and attention is constantly demanded. A person in a precarious labour situation may experience the future as radically shortened because planning beyond the immediate horizon feels implausible. A young adult in a transitional stage of life may inhabit several conflicting temporalities at once: their own slower process of becoming, the accelerated timelines of peers, the expectations of family, and the deadlines imposed by institutions. These conditions enter coaching because they shape what kinds of reflection and action are realistic.</p>"},"id":"cf409069-5d63-405e-9a8c-4f20650b8878","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Embodiment is central throughout. Temporal experience is registered in and through the body: acceleration, fatigue, agitation, slowness, anticipation, and suspension are bodily states as much as cognitive interpretations. A person who feels pressed for time often also speaks more quickly, breathes more shallowly, and moves differently through space. A person living in prolonged uncertainty may carry that uncertainty somatically as tension, insomnia, or diffuse exhaustion. In coaching, attending to temporal experience therefore includes attending to rhythms of speech, pace of movement, pauses, hesitations, and the body’s relation to surroundings. Presence, in this sense, involves more than psychological attentiveness. It concerns the way coach and coachee co-constitute a spatial-temporal setting in which a different rhythm of perception and articulation becomes possible.</p>"},"id":"ef05f85f-e4fd-481d-be6a-991de3192c8e","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>This has consequences for how I understand the coaching session itself. A session is a bounded temporal event, yet it also has its own atmosphere, pace, and duration. The arrangement of the room, the distance between bodies, the sequence of attention, the tolerance for silence, and the way transitions are handled all contribute to its temporal quality. Some settings encourage haste and instrumental problem-solving. Others allow for a slower unfolding in which associations, memories, and unrealised possibilities can surface. The spatial organisation of the session participates in the temporal experience of the conversation. Seating, posture, visual focus, and interruptions all influence whether time feels compressed, suspended, or sufficiently open for reflection.</p>"},"id":"63aaf465-d136-4089-a51e-73e50546c2ee","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>In practice, these ideas lead me to ask how a person’s life is organised in space and time together. I am interested in the temporal architecture of a typical day, week, or transition period, and in the places through which these rhythms are carried. We may look at where time feels fragmented, where it thickens, where waiting accumulates, where recovery is possible, and where different temporal demands collide. This can reveal that difficulties attributed to indecision or lack of discipline are often linked to a more complex spatial-temporal organisation of life. Coaching can then support experiments that address tempo as well as thought: altering routines, protecting slower spaces, changing the sequence of environments across the day, or creating more viable intervals between demanding activities. Such adjustments can modify how a person experiences agency because they reshape the temporal and spatial conditions under which agency is exercised.</p>"},"id":"7a80e686-059e-452d-b94d-aa4742a0280a","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>For me, phenomenological accounts of temporality have been valuable because they connect biography, embodiment, and environment in a single field of analysis. Coaching informed by this perspective can attend more precisely to how time is felt, how it is spatially organised, and how small changes in rhythm and setting can alter what becomes thinkable and possible.</p>"},"id":"9f09953e-9ca0-4d4d-bf6a-418575b4d370","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
