The 18–25 age range is marked by transitions that are both structurally significant and internally diffuse: choices about study, work, relationships, identity, and location are often made under conditions of uncertainty and incomplete information. I recognise many of these dynamics from my decades in higher education, yet coaching allows me to engage with them in a different, more individually focused way.</p>"},"id":"69882918-2086-4bf4-a7bc-d29c0b10cfb4","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>My teaching experience has given me a long view on how young adults think, hesitate, experiment, and revise their paths. I have seen several generations move through similar developmental questions in very different social and technological landscapes. This perspective helps me distinguish between what is specific to a particular historical moment and what belongs to recurring patterns of early adulthood. It also supports a certain patience: I am less inclined to treat indecision or change of direction as failure, and more as part of a longer process of orientation.</p>"},"id":"dfd34e03-34ff-4db2-aa9b-f90d6aa1da38","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Coaching with young adults benefits from this background in at least two ways. I recognise how seriousness and play, ambition and doubt, can coexist in unstable configurations. Second, I understand the institutional environments they often inhabit—universities, early-career workplaces, training programmes—and the explicit and implicit demands these contexts impose.</p>"},"id":"5cf2eaca-8c27-4f8a-8153-8eaf22afed35","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>What I value in this work is the possibility of creating a space that is not primarily evaluative. In educational settings, young adults are frequently assessed, graded, and compared. Coaching offers a different kind of interaction, one in which their questions do not have to converge on a single correct answer. My role is less to provide direction and more to support the development of their own criteria for what a viable and meaningful next step might look like.</p>"},"id":"98e32503-7f0e-45ad-98c1-de03b0df61fe","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Over time, I have found that young adults respond well to this combination of experience and openness. This allows for conversations that can hold both the weight of long-term consequences and the experimental character of this phase of life. It is in this intersection—between teaching and coaching, between structure and exploration—that I find the work with young adults particularly rewarding.</p>"},"id":"5d317c82-1735-4de2-bcef-66ba394d75c9","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
