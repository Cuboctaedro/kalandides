Body: [{"content":{"text":"<p>Most discussions of urban health focus on housing, transport, employment, or access to services. Less often considered is the sensory climate in which urban life unfolds: the continuous presence of noise and artificial light that surrounds daily routines. These elements are easy to overlook because they are so pervasive, yet they have significant effects on how bodies function, how people sleep, and how attention and mood are regulated.</p>"},"id":"944098dd-3acd-43b5-ac98-bce176ba1bda","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Urban noise is seldom experienced as a single dramatic disturbance. It appears as a constant background of traffic, construction, voices, music, mechanical systems, and electronic signals. In many areas, this background never fully recedes. The nervous system remains in a state of low-level alertness, continually processing sounds that may indicate threat or demand a response. Over time, this ongoing activation affects sleep quality, cardiovascular regulation, and thresholds for irritation. People often describe themselves as being permanently “on edge,” without necessarily connecting this to the acoustic conditions in which they live.</p>"},"id":"f3239a9c-cff5-4e86-a396-daf5afe398d3","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Artificial light introduces a parallel dimension. Street lighting, illuminated advertisements, car headlights, screens, and interior lighting extend brightness well into the night. Human physiological rhythms have developed in relation to cycles of light and dark. Extended exposure to light in the evening, particularly light with a strong blue component, interferes with the hormonal processes that prepare the body for sleep and cellular repair. Falling asleep becomes more difficult, sleep becomes more superficial, and waking is less restorative. The consequences are visible in fatigue, altered mood, reduced cognitive performance, and long-term strain on bodily systems.</p>"},"id":"446c2532-a445-47a8-a136-db949095e4f4","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Public awareness of these processes remains limited. Noise and light are often framed as matters of personal sensitivity or lifestyle preference. Complaints about them are easily dismissed as a lack of resilience or unwillingness to accept the conditions of contemporary urban life. Yet the mechanisms by which chronic noise exposure and disrupted light–dark cycles influence health are well established. The gap between this knowledge and the way cities are planned, regulated, and inhabited remains striking.</p>"},"id":"aa722c1c-f653-4d04-a795-c8c01cfe3597","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>The burdens of adverse sensory environments are not distributed evenly. Housing near major roads, airports, industrial zones, or entertainment districts carries higher levels of noise and intrusive light. Such housing is often more affordable, and therefore more likely to be occupied by lower-income households, migrants, and other vulnerable groups. Poorer construction quality amplifies the problem, as thin walls and inadequate windows allow sound and light to enter more easily. For many residents, options for retreating to quieter, darker environments are limited by financial constraints and by obligations that keep them tied to particular locations.</p>"},"id":"986f96ef-3794-4a91-862e-838f1ae1b23f","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>The sensory climate also shapes how public spaces are experienced. Some streets are so dominated by traffic noise that holding a conversation requires effort. Others allow a mixed soundscape in which human voices, birds, and moderate background sounds coexist. Night-time lighting is sometimes so intense that it prevents any sense of enclosure or rest; in other places, lighting is so sparse or uneven that it generates fear rather than calm. For women, queer people, migrants, and others at higher risk of harassment or violence, these conditions become part of everyday safety calculations. A very noisy environment can make it harder to register warning signals or to call for help. An environment that is brightly lit but poorly designed can increase feelings of exposure without necessarily improving security.</p>"},"id":"cbf48973-5fdd-4a1a-93d4-082d1f225498","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Inside homes, people encounter similar issues. Shared walls transmit the sounds of neighbours and common areas. Stairwells and courtyards bring voices and footsteps into private rooms. Street lighting or commercial signage may infiltrate bedrooms, even when curtains are closed. Attempts to create small zones of quiet and darkness often require resources for better insulation, relocation, or specialised equipment. Those without such resources live in a state of ongoing adaptation to sensory conditions that do not support basic cycles of rest and restoration.</p>"},"id":"99be249e-eb39-48bf-941e-fa33b836a6d5","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Treating noise and light as components of urban infrastructure would alter how they are addressed. Questions would shift from individual coping to collective arrangements. Where are quiet zones feasible? How can lighting be organised to balance safety with the need for darkness at night? Which populations are most exposed to harmful levels of noise and artificial light, and what forms of regulation, building standards, or support could modify this distribution? These are not peripheral considerations. They touch on the environments within which bodies and minds attempt to maintain themselves day after day, and they illustrate how the health of urban inhabitants is shaped as much by sensory conditions as by more visible aspects of city life.</p>"},"id":"50fc7e3e-1536-4013-9cdc-d4de7a0cdc22","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
