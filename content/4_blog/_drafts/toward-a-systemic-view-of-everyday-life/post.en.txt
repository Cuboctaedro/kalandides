Systems theory offers a language for patterns, relationships, contexts, and the feedback loops through which situations sustain themselves over time, instead of a language centred on isolated behaviours, intentions, or decisions taken once and for all. A comment at work feeds into an interpretation of one’s role, this interpretation shapes how one speaks in the next meeting, and that next interaction then confirms the original perception. A person who feels uncertain in social situations may withdraw slightly, the withdrawal leads others to engage less, and the diminished engagement confirms the sense of not belonging, so the pattern strengthens. What appears as a stable trait - “I am not good in groups”— -often rests on a loop in which behaviour, environment, and interpretation continually amplify one another and stabilise a particular way of being.</p>"},"id":"82a24851-69ac-4151-bfc5-40d07dfec649","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Coaching conversations take on a different texture when approached from this systemic angle. Feedback loops become a central object of inquiry: which actions lead to which responses, how those responses shape further expectations, and how this entire sequence stabilises certain patterns of life. Where in this web of relations is an intervention possible that the system can register? Attention, timing, and relationship gain importance in this framing. A gesture that passes unnoticed in one phase of a loop may become transformative in another, because the surrounding conditions create a different readiness for change. The person acts from within the system, and their actions reverberate through relational and material structures that respond in specific, sometimes unexpected, ways.</p>"},"id":"29b2e689-6156-4f0b-9987-085f0311b55d","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>From a systemic perspective, apparently internal experiences - such as self-doubt, motivation, or a sense of depletion - can be read as signals of how wider arrangements are functioning. Motivation rises and falls in response to whether feedback from the environment acknowledges effort or whether work disappears into opaque procedures. Fatigue often reflects a loop in which long commutes, noisy or unstable housing, and unbounded work hours reinforce each other. Within this map, feedback loops appear as sites of potential reconfiguration. A coachee may experiment with a modest shift - changing the rhythm of their day, altering how they respond to a recurring demand, or renegotiating a boundary - and then observe how the system responds, using that response as information for the next step. Change often travels along these loops, where a small, well-placed shift can gradually reshape a pattern, while large efforts that ignore systemic connections dissipate without lasting effect. A systemic view of everyday life offers a framework that is slower and more demanding than simple linear explanations, yet it provides a more precise way of understanding why some patterns persist and where room for movement may still exist.</p>"},"id":"d46f1163-e90f-464c-aa9c-fbd94cfa871a","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
