Title: Spaces of encounter, inequality, and social isolation

Body: [{"content":{"text":"<p>In earlier pieces, I have written about cities and the health of their inhabitants, about housing and psychological stability, and about green spaces and restoration. Another dimension of urban life that matters for health is the presence and quality of spaces of encounter. Streets, cafés, squares, parks, and public transport are not only infrastructures for movement and consumption. They are places where people see and are seen, where brief recognitions occur, and where a sense of living among others is formed. When these spaces function well, they help prevent deep isolation. When they are missing, hostile, or governed by restrictive norms, they contribute to loneliness and stress.</p>"},"id":"cac055eb-be9c-4653-82ec-adc95cc4a32f","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Spaces of encounter support forms of low-threshold sociability. A nod to a neighbour, a short exchange with a shopkeeper, standing in line with others, or sharing a bench with strangers does not usually develop into close relationship, yet it contributes to the feeling of having a place in a shared social world. These small contacts offer a background sense that other lives are unfolding alongside one’s own, that one is not entirely alone. For many people, especially those living alone or working in fragmented conditions, such micro-encounters make a difference to mood and to how a day feels.</p>"},"id":"a86f2ac0-4f31-4c01-83d7-05e8ea573c89","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>The physical distribution and design of these spaces vary widely across a city. Some neighbourhoods contain walkable streets, local cafés, small shops, and public squares that invite lingering. Others are dominated by busy roads, car parks, and enclosed buildings with little provision for staying. In the latter environments, people move quickly from one interior to another, and public space becomes mainly a transit corridor. The opportunities for informal encounter diminish, and social life depends more heavily on private invitations, organised activities, or commercial venues, which are harder to access for those with limited time, money, or confidence.</p>"},"id":"29475b3f-8fa3-4455-8ee8-956613877895","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Rules of inclusion and exclusion are always present in these spaces, even when they are not written down. Certain cafés, squares, or parks implicitly answer the question “Who belongs here?” by the way they are furnished, priced, policed, and populated. In my country of origine, Greece, for example, village cafés have mostly functioned informally as male spaces. There is no law banning women, yet the pattern of use, the expectations around who sits there and for how long, and the reactions to women’s presence produce a boundary that many women experience as real. Similar informal rules operate elsewhere. Same-sex couples may find that affection or even simple gestures of closeness attract stares or comments in some settings, while passing without notice in others. People racialised as outsiders may be watched more closely, questioned, or made to feel unwelcome through small but cumulative signals. These mechanisms shape who actually uses spaces of encounter and who avoids them.</p>"},"id":"6311316c-6f83-4567-9a28-a0e9caaef165","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Such rules are enforced through many small practices: who gets served promptly and who waits, where staff look, how security personnel behave, which groups are asked to move on, and how people react to different bodies and appearances. Over time, those who encounter repeated signals of non-belonging adjust their routes and habits. They restrict their presence to spaces where they feel relatively safe or at least less exposed, even if this means longer travel, reduced access to amenities, or a narrower field of movement. The city may appear full of potential places to sit, walk, and meet, yet for them the actually usable map is much smaller.</p>"},"id":"7c037d4f-eb88-4b5a-b944-f6cb4ff5176a","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>When spaces of encounter are limited, difficult to access, or marked by exclusionary norms, social isolation gains a spatial form. People may spend most of their time either at home or in functionally specialised settings such as workplaces, shops, and institutions. The intermediate spaces where one can be in public without performing a role become rare. For those who lack strong private networks, this absence can lead to a kind of solitude that is not immediately visible: life takes place largely indoors or in transit, and the city’s public life is something observed from a distance rather than inhabited.</p>"},"id":"e7d7c04d-e9aa-498e-b9c2-dc2f3105fcc4","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Thinking about health in this context means asking where, in a given city, people can be present without being primarily consumers, clients, or suspects. It involves examining which groups feel welcome in particular spaces, which enter them with caution, and which mostly stay away. It also means paying attention to the modest material conditions that support or undermine encounter: the presence of benches at no cost, the width and continuity of pavements, the availability of shade, the level of traffic, and the design of public transport that allows people to share space without excessive discomfort or fear.</p>"},"id":"e5da709c-7e10-493a-8cb4-f887b6801034","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Spaces of encounter do not dissolve structural inequalities or guarantee meaningful relationships. They form part of the background conditions under which people experience themselves as part of a shared world. When these conditions are more favourable and more evenly distributed, it becomes easier to sustain everyday contact and recognition, even in periods of personal difficulty. When they are absent or governed by informal rules of exclusion, the burden of creating social life falls heavily on individuals, many of whom already live with heightened levels of stress, uncertainty, or stigma.</p>"},"id":"91df9997-48b8-4da8-b9e4-d21d9263a23f","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
