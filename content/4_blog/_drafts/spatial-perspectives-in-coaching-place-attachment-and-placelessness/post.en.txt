Body: [{"content":{"text":"<p>This text forms the second part of a series on spatial perspectives in coaching. The first outlined why space matters in my work and how phenomenological accounts of lived space and the body inform my practice. Here the focus shifts to humanistic geography, and in particular to the work of Yi-Fu Tuan and Edward Relph, who have developed influential concepts of place, sense of place, attachment, insideness, outsideness, and placelessness. These ideas provide a language for phenomena that I encounter frequently in coaching: feeling at home, feeling out of place, losing attachment to a familiar environment, or inhabiting standardised settings that seem interchangeable.</p>"},"id":"d85d2039-f118-4160-93df-9e5624232997","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Humanistic geography treats place not as a purely physical location, but as space that has been endowed with meaning through experience. In Tuan’s formulation, space becomes place as it is lived in, named, remembered, and narrated. A street, an office, or a neighbourhood does not become a place simply by existing; it becomes a place when people associate it with particular events, routines, emotions, and stories. “Sense of place” refers to this complex of attachments and evaluations. It includes comfort, familiarity, and affection, but also unease, boredom, or fear. Places can feel safe or threatening, enlivening or dull, hospitable or hostile. In coaching, I notice that when people speak of being “in the wrong place” in their work or personal lives, they are often referring to a disturbance in this sense of place.</p>"},"id":"13da1d8d-33a2-4792-ab90-f44a811bb767","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Relph’s analysis of insideness and outsideness deepens this understanding. Existential insideness describes a mode of being in which a person feels thoroughly at home in a place: the environment is taken for granted, its rhythms are familiar, and one’s own presence there feels unquestioned. Outsideness, by contrast, describes distance, alienation, or non-belonging. A person may be physically located somewhere yet feel that they are not really “in” the place, that they remain on its margins or are merely passing through. This distinction is useful in coaching when coachees report that they cannot “settle” in a new role, city, or organisation, despite having all the formal markers of membership. The issue is not simply adaptation to tasks, but the absence of existential insideness.</p>"},"id":"21bb1135-70b3-4a0c-8678-882fb5ad4a73","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Relph also introduces the notion of placelessness to describe environments that have lost or never acquired distinctive character. Standardised commercial zones, interchangeable office complexes, and homogenised urban developments can produce a sense that one might be anywhere and nowhere at the same time. This does not mean that people cannot function in such spaces, but it can make attachment more difficult. In coaching, I encounter this when people describe their workplaces or even their cities as “generic,” “anonymous,” or “empty of atmosphere.” They often link this to a sense of fatigue or detachment that is hard to explain purely in terms of workload or interpersonal dynamics.</p>"},"id":"5a25f6e7-d9b6-4ddf-8221-401b5cc5973b","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>These concepts from humanistic geography inform my coaching practice in several ways. First, they invite me to ask concrete questions about where coachees feel inside or outside, where they experience belonging and where they experience estrangement. When someone speaks about dissatisfaction at work, we explore not only tasks and relationships, but also whether the workplace feels like a place to them at all, or whether it remains a placeless setting they move through without attachment. This can reveal that what is experienced as a lack of motivation is partly a lack of sense of place: the environment does not support identification or investment.</p>"},"id":"033c9ce7-524d-4786-ad3b-4c52a2c122d4","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Second, Tuan’s emphasis on narrative and memory leads me to pay attention to the stories people tell about the places they have inhabited over time. A former neighbourhood that is recalled with warmth, a childhood home that is described as oppressive, a city that once symbolised opportunity and now feels depleted—these narratives show how place and self are co-constituted. In coaching, mapping a person’s “place biography” can clarify how their current evaluations of environments are shaped by earlier experiences. For instance, a strong attachment to a particular type of landscape or urban form may not be arbitrary, but rooted in formative periods of life. Recognising these connections can make present choices about relocation, workplace, or housing more intelligible.</p>"},"id":"7b5910db-9457-42b3-a5df-b388fc45ab3a","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Third, Relph’s distinction between insideness and outsideness provides a way to frame feelings of displacement without immediately psychologising them as individual shortcomings. When a coachee feels chronically out of place in an organisation, it may be tempting to focus exclusively on their beliefs, skills, or interpersonal strategies. Bringing in the notion of outsideness allows us to ask whether the institution, its culture, and its spatial configuration actually afford the possibility of becoming an insider. In some cases, the environment structurally positions certain people, roles, or identities at the periphery. Coaching can then address both how the person wishes to respond to this positioning and whether remaining in that environment is a viable option.</p>"},"id":"7ae7aaec-0b25-4412-b8f0-91bbdacd977f","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Finally, the idea of placelessness offers a lens for understanding certain forms of exhaustion. When people move between settings that are heavily standardised—similar offices, airports, hotels, digital interfaces—they may lose opportunities for grounded attachment. The days blur, and locations cease to provide orientation or meaning. In such situations, coaching can include exploring where and how a stronger sense of place might be reintroduced. This does not necessarily require dramatic changes. It can involve cultivating small, specific places in daily life that have recognisable character and personal significance: a particular bench in a park, a corner of a room arranged with care, a route that becomes familiar and valued. These micro-places can function as anchors in an otherwise placeless landscape.</p>"},"id":"9fd53693-1dbb-4257-befd-b2286a9a35a8","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Working with place, attachment, and placelessness in coaching thus means taking seriously the ways in which environments are more than backdrops for psychological processes. They are part of how identity, motivation, and belonging are formed and sustained. By drawing on humanistic geography, I can ask not only what people do and think, but where they feel at home, where they feel excluded, and where they feel as if they could be anywhere. These questions often open areas of reflection and action that remain inaccessible if attention is restricted to internal states or abstract descriptions of roles and tasks.</p>"},"id":"bc9484de-23fa-46aa-9e1b-d3c08d05cc12","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
