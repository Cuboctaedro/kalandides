Earlier pieces introduced the general rationale for the series and then turned to phenomenology and humanistic geography. Here the emphasis is on relational and critical approaches to space, above all on the work of Doreen Massey, whose thinking has been a major influence on me for many years. Her account of space as relational, open, and constituted through power-laden social interactions has shaped my research in geography and continues to inform how I understand the settings in which coaching takes place.</p>"},"id":"dd663976-84fc-4acd-b936-fdf69bcf8657","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>For Massey, space is produced through relations. A street, a neighbourhood, a town - even our world - comes into being through movements, interactions, institutional arrangements, and unequal positions that evolve over time. Places are not self-contained units with intrinsic properties. They are configurations of trajectories and practices that intersect and stabilise for a while. This understanding directs attention away from fixed spatial containers and toward the changing networks through which spaces and institutions are made.</p>"},"id":"e367490c-15d8-4b76-b5ab-0166f056663e","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>A central point in Massey’s work is that spatial arrangements play an active role in the maintenance and transformation of social relations. Social processes generate spatial forms, and these forms in turn organise who meets whom, who is visible, who can withdraw, and who controls resources and information. The design of a building, the zoning of a city, or the layout of a workplace participates in the distribution of mobility, exposure, and authority. Spatial form becomes one of the media through which power operates and through which hierarchies, gendered expectations, and other inequalities are held in place or begin to shift. In my own geographical research, this reciprocal relation between social processes and spatial configuration has been a guiding theme.</p>"},"id":"4e0d11ac-4c93-49c1-b745-fa09ad5a3576","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Critical human geography and critical phenomenology of social space add further nuance by examining how space is lived from different positions. The same office, university, or public square is encountered differently depending on gender, sexuality, class, race, age, and bodily presentation. Some people experience a setting as open and permissive, others as policed or demanding. Access, mobility, and exposure are organised unevenly, and this unevenness is perceived and responded to in everyday movement and behaviour. Many of the themes that arise in my coaching work—masculinity, sexual orientation, vulnerability, belonging, visibility—are closely tied to these differentiated experiences of space.</p>"},"id":"f15ccd1d-abe9-421c-a603-5eed9516382f","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>These perspectives shape my coaching practice in several ways. When a coachee describes inhibition, marginality, or pressure, I ask how the spatial organisation of their environment participates in that experience. The focus is not limited to workplaces. I am interested in how their home is arranged and inhabited, how privacy and exposure are distributed in domestic spaces, which rooms feel available to them and which feel claimed by others. I ask about neighbourhoods and towns, about the routes they take, the public spaces they use or avoid, and the kinds of encounters that are likely in those settings. Communities and groups—formal and informal—also have spatial forms: meeting places, gathering points, boundaries that separate insiders from outsiders.</p>"},"id":"cf3ff28d-f334-44b3-88d0-d6702138f0ae","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Questions about office layout, seating in meetings, circulation routes, and locations of informal conversations are one part of this inquiry. Questions about how a kitchen, living room, or bedroom is used, how far a person travels to meet friends, where they feel watched in their own building or street, and where they feel invisible are another part. The aim is to understand how authority, recognition, and exclusion are enacted through concrete arrangements of walls, doors, tables, thresholds, and streets, as well as through patterns of distance, proximity, and access in wider environments. This reduces the tendency to interpret all difficulty as a matter of individual deficit and brings into view the relational field in which a life is situated: the overlapping spaces of home, work, neighbourhood, and community that together structure what feels possible.</p>"},"id":"d3aeda40-2b50-4523-987b-531a18d4c52a","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Such interventions do not aim to solve structural problems on their own. They create altered conditions under which different forms of interaction can emerge. Changing who sits next to whom, choosing alternative rooms for certain discussions, organising meetings in less formal settings, or adjusting lines of sight and proximity in everyday work can modify who encounters whom, who feels able to speak, and how authority is expressed. These adjustments do not replace work on roles, expectations, or internalised norms. They create situations in which new relational configurations can be tested and observed.</p>"},"id":"e0bcc9d5-fd95-464f-a99d-7db304146235","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>For individual coachees, attending to relational space and power can clarify why certain contexts feel consistently draining or unsafe. A young man may recognise that specific peer environments reward narrow performances of masculinity and discourage other ways of being. A person from a marginalised background may become aware that particular professional spaces were not designed with their presence in mind. These recognitions can inform decisions about where to invest energy in adaptation, where to seek alternative spaces of support, and where withdrawal or exit is a legitimate option.</p>"},"id":"be9fb5f2-f9ad-43f7-aff7-2d85b6bfab10","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Working with Massey’s conception of relational space in coaching means treating environments as active elements in the situations people bring. It involves asking how spatial configurations stabilise existing patterns of interaction and where modest changes might open room for different experiences of participation, voice, and connection. This line of thinking extends my work as a geographer into my coaching practice by keeping questions of power, inequality, and possibility present whenever I consider the spaces in which people live and work.</p>"},"id":"be3fe6a6-8e17-4c31-a925-db6f66663fe6","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
