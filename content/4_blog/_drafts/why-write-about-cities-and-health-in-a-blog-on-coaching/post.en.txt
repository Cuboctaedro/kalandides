These conditions influence sleep, stress, movement, social contact, and the ability to think clearly. Coaching is also concerned with these capacities, even when it uses different language. I do not understand people as isolated individuals who happen to have some external circumstances. I think of them as always already embedded in systems. These systems include family structures, organisations, economic conditions, but also material structures such as houses, streets, transport networks, and public spaces. Cities are not neutral containers around lives. They are active elements in how lives are lived. They participate in producing stress or relief, isolation or connection, possibility or constriction.</p> <p></p>"},"id":"076d37e4-782f-402c-8c51-db10c948fad1","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>When I work as a coach, I therefore pay attention to systems and structures as well as to inner experience. I am interested in how a person’s difficulties and possibilities are organised by their position within larger arrangements. This involves institutional systems, but also spatial ones. A person living in precarious housing in a noisy, poorly connected neighbourhood experiences their own agency differently from someone living in a stable, quiet, well-served area. A young queer person who must calculate their safety in public space inhabits the city differently from someone who moves without such concerns. These differences matter when we talk about what is realistic, what is changeable, and what it means to take care of oneself.</p>"},"id":"e3771b69-aa49-44f3-afd8-726d038da202","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>The series on cities and health is a way of making these connections more visible. It sets out, in a more general form, some of the background against which many coaching conversations take place. It also reflects a conviction that individual work on wellbeing should not obscure the role of collective, structural, and spatial conditions. Coaching can help people navigate their situations more consciously. It cannot, on its own, redesign the city. Yet understanding how urban space participates in health can prevent us from interpreting every difficulty as a private failure and can open questions about what changes are needed beyond the individual.</p>"},"id":"f9aa1e92-1ae1-4a4e-ad7b-48e79ea0c87f","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Ending the series with this reflection is therefore less about closing a topic and more about marking a link. They underline that when we speak about stress, fatigue, motivation, or belonging, we are also speaking about housing, streets, parks, noise, light, safety, and planning decisions that were made long before any particular coaching conversation began.</p>"},"id":"ce4ed662-8b86-4434-9ab5-2c93700fce7a","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
