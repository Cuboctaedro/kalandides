Even in contexts where legal and social conditions have improved, earlier experiences of concealment or hostility continue to inform how they assess relational risk.</p>"},"id":"0ed34b56-6f35-4d25-b5d9-f57a4d22f513","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>This shows up in topics that on the surface look unrelated to sexual orientation. The body remembers the conditions under which it felt unsafe, and adapts accordingly.</p>"},"id":"95ae8106-5db1-44a2-8a38-575907e1b968","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>In coaching, I try to treat these adaptations with respect. This requires a careful mapping of contexts, rather than a generalised encouragement to “be more open” or “stop hiding.”</p>"},"id":"45ff6712-4a6a-4aa3-945c-f1147100dedb","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>At the same time, it is important not to romanticise visibility. The aim is not maximal disclosure, but agency: to choose, rather than be compelled by fear or external expectations, how visible one wishes to be.</p>"},"id":"359c8702-6b75-4d1b-bba8-638bd688da6b","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
