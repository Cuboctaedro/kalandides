We understand others through socially available categories, biographies, roles, and sedimented assumptions about how people like them and people like us are likely to act. Intersubjectivity, in this sense, does not refer only to moments of explicit mutual understanding. It refers to the whole socially organised field through which people become intelligible to one another and through which relations are made possible or difficult. There are also encounters in which the other is addressed as a presence rather than reduced to a function. This distinction has often been taken up in ways that are overly abstract, but I find it useful when thinking about the “space between” people. The relational space of an interaction shifts according to whether the other person is being handled, assessed, interpreted, tolerated, or genuinely encountered. It includes patterns of expectation about what will happen between us, what kinds of response are available, what will be recognised, what will be ignored, and what may become costly to say. Some people enter the conversation expecting evaluation. Others anticipate distance, benevolent neutrality, excessive intimacy, or the need to perform coherence. These anticipations have biographies behind them, and they are linked to the social worlds the person inhabits outside coaching: family systems, workplaces, communities, friendships, and intimate relationships. The coaching relationship becomes meaningful partly because it offers a differently organised intersubjective space in which some of these expectations can be confirmed, revised, or loosened.</p>"},"id":"53955c99-e35d-4834-96a5-38784f81b0a3","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>For this reason, I pay attention not only to what is said but to how the relational field is forming as we speak. I am interested in rhythm, interruption, hesitation, eye contact, distance, silence, forms of address, and changes in tone. I am attentive to when the conversation becomes more exploratory and when it contracts into performance or self-protection. This is relevant with all coachees, but it has particular importance in areas I often work with, including masculinity, sexual orientation, vulnerability, and the developmental tensions of young adulthood. The “space between” people is never neutral. It is shaped by gendered expectations, by previous experiences of shame or recognition, by generational difference, and by the perceived risks of disclosure.</p>"},"id":"bf3517d9-d0b6-4da6-8e6e-5ea80021c882","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Schutz helps me think about how coachees arrive with socially sedimented typifications of who a coach is and what kind of relationship this will be. Bringing these assumptions into awareness can loosen their hold. Buber helps me think about the quality of presence required if coaching is to become a space in which the person is encountered with seriousness rather than reduced to a case or a problem. A coaching conversation can become a space where norms of evaluation are temporarily suspended, where contradictory material can remain in view without immediate resolution, and where forms of language that are unavailable elsewhere begin to emerge.
