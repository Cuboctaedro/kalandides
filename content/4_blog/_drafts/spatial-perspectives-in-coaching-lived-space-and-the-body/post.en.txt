Here the focus is on phenomenological approaches that treat space as lived, embodied, and meaningful. The work of Merleau-Ponty, Bachelard, and Lefebvre has been particularly important in giving me a vocabulary for what I often hear in coaching when people describe feeling exposed, constrained, or at home in specific environments.</p>"},"id":"376d59ec-770c-4b01-8fff-07264ad0fd3a","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Merleau-Ponty’s account of the lived body offers a starting point. He understands the body not as an object located in space, but as the very condition of spatial experience. The notion of a body schema captures the pre-reflective organisation of posture, movement, and orientation: a tacit sense of where one’s body is, what it can reach, and how it can act in a given situation. From this perspective, space is always already structured by what the body can and cannot do. A meeting room is not simply a set of coordinates; it is a field of possible gestures, positions, and paths. When a coachee consistently chooses the same seat, avoids certain areas, or feels unable to speak in particular configurations, this can be read through Merleau-Ponty as an expression of how their body schema has adapted to a specific spatial order.</p>"},"id":"c8b3eedf-ea76-47d1-a6a0-29360bb76859","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Bachelard’s exploration of intimate spaces adds another layer. In his work on the poetics of space, he shows how seemingly trivial locations—corners, staircases, attics, cellars—are invested with memory, fantasy, and affect. These are spaces of withdrawal, dreaming, or fear. They are less about function and more about shelter, exposure, and imagination. In coaching, I find that when people speak about where they can think, rest, or reflect, they often refer to similarly small and patterned spaces: a particular chair, a balcony, a stretch of pavement, a habitual corner in a café. Bachelard’s attention to these “nests” and retreats helps make sense of why small spatial changes can have disproportionate effects on mood and capacity.</p>"},"id":"e752e72b-b963-4472-9096-0c7af41113b5","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Lefebvre’s distinction between representations of space and spatial practice further clarifies the gap between official layouts and lived experience. Representations of space are plans, maps, organisational charts: the abstract ways in which institutions conceive and design environments. Spatial practice refers to how people actually move, gather, avoid, and inhabit these environments in everyday life. Lived space, in his terms, is where symbolic meanings, power relations, and affective investments are condensed. It is in lived space that hierarchies become visible and invisible, that routes of access are opened or closed, and that certain bodies are made to feel legitimate or marginal. When a coachee describes never using a particular entrance, always passing through the same corridor, or preferring transitional spaces over central ones, Lefebvre’s account of spatial practice offers a way to interpret these patterns as responses to a produced social space rather than as idiosyncrasies.</p>"},"id":"dcbcb646-c47d-460b-8772-29be5fc22011","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>These theoretical strands inform my coaching in several ways. First, they encourage me to treat spatial descriptions as substantive data rather than as incidental context. When someone says they feel “cornered” in their role, I do not assume this is only a metaphor. I ask which actual corners they occupy, how their desk is positioned, where they sit in meetings, what lines of sight and lines of movement surround them. Merleau-Ponty’s emphasis on the body schema leads me to inquire into posture, reach, and distance: how close others are, who is behind them, where exits are located. This often reveals that a sense of constraint or hypervigilance is reinforced daily by a particular spatial organisation that channels attention and narrows perceived options.</p>"},"id":"3d2f4e20-b766-4582-95c0-c56766283bb3","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Second, Bachelard’s focus on intimate space prompts me to ask about the small, recurrent environments where coachees find relief or agitation. We talk about their habitual places for work, rest, or thinking, and about the spatial histories attached to them. A kitchen table associated with family conflict, a childhood room that no longer exists, a city street that has become a site of anxiety—all of these can structure current possibilities for concentration or calm. This leads to very concrete experiments: identifying or creating new “nests” for reflection, deliberately shifting certain activities to spaces that carry less emotional residue, or recognising that some environments are so saturated with memory that expecting them to support new habits is unrealistic.</p>"},"id":"34b5e3ce-5809-43a9-adae-4788e3091ff4","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Third, Lefebvre’s analysis of lived social space makes me attentive to how institutional power is stabilised through spatial arrangements. In organisational coaching, I often ask for a description or sketch of the workplace: who occupies enclosed offices, who is visible in open-plan areas, where informal encounters happen, where one can remain unobserved. This helps situate individual struggles within a spatialised hierarchy. A person’s reluctance to speak in meetings may be connected to a seating plan that literally places them at the edge of the room; a feeling of constant surveillance may be linked to transparent walls or open sightlines rather than only to internalised anxiety. Recognising these spatial dimensions can shift the focus from self-blame to a more accurate assessment of constraints and affordances.</p>"},"id":"5cac0065-4725-41c0-b781-5a15cbee7ef9","isHidden":false,"type":"text"},{"content":{"text":"<p>Working with these ideas in coaching often leads to a practice of spatial diagnosis and experimentation. We reconstruct a typical day in spatial terms: where the person is, how they move between locations, what bodily states accompany those transitions. We then look for points where small modifications might alter the configuration of lived space: changing the route to work, renegotiating seating in recurring meetings, adjusting the arrangement of objects on a desk, planning conversations in different settings. These interventions are modest, yet they make a difference because they address the level at which, in Merleau-Ponty’s terms, the body understands its world pre-reflectively. They also acknowledge, with Bachelard and Lefebvre, that spaces are not interchangeable containers but storied, structured environments that participate in the production of subjectivity. In this way, phenomenological accounts of lived space and the body offer both an interpretive framework and a set of practical tools for working with the spatial dimensions of what coachees bring.</p>"},"id":"c4f63179-8b2b-40f7-a2ee-e093549a0a1f","isHidden":false,"type":"text"}]
